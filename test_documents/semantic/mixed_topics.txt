The Pacific Ocean covers approximately 165 million square kilometers, making
it the largest body of water on Earth. It stretches from the Arctic in the
north to Antarctica in the south, and from Asia and Australia in the west to
the Americas in the east. The Mariana Trench, located in the western Pacific,
reaches a depth of nearly 11,000 meters at its deepest point, Challenger Deep.

Ocean temperatures vary widely by region and depth. Surface waters near the
equator average around 27 degrees Celsius, while polar regions hover near
freezing. The thermocline, a layer of rapid temperature change, typically
occurs between 200 and 1,000 meters depth and separates warm surface waters
from the cold deep ocean.

The French Revolution began in 1789 with the storming of the Bastille on
July 14. What started as a fiscal crisis under King Louis XVI rapidly
escalated into a radical restructuring of French society. The Declaration of
the Rights of Man and of the Citizen, adopted in August 1789, established
principles of liberty, equality, and popular sovereignty that would influence
democratic movements worldwide.

The Reign of Terror, lasting from September 1793 to July 1794, saw
thousands executed under the Committee of Public Safety led by Maximilien
Robespierre. The period ended with Robespierre's own arrest and execution
in the Thermidorian Reaction. Napoleon Bonaparte's rise to power followed,
culminating in his coronation as Emperor in 1804.

Photosynthesis is the process by which green plants convert sunlight into
chemical energy. Chlorophyll molecules in the thylakoid membranes of
chloroplasts absorb light primarily in the blue and red wavelengths,
reflecting green light and giving plants their characteristic color.

The Calvin cycle, occurring in the stroma of chloroplasts, uses the ATP and
NADPH produced by the light reactions to fix carbon dioxide into
three-carbon sugar molecules. These sugars are then used to build glucose
and other organic compounds that fuel the plant's metabolism and growth.
